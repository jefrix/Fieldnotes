The Frozen
Conflict

Sixty-two days into the war. Four clocks are now running. Every one of them has slowed down. The system is reorganizing around the damage; that is what a frozen conflict looks like.

Published April 30, 2026
Reading time ~26 minutes
Filed under Geopolitics / Economics
Sequel to №01, №05, №11

Two days ago, this publication argued that the Iran war had crossed the line from a shock into a condition. The Strait of Hormuz had become a stalemate; a dual blockade in which neither side could open the strait without losing leverage, and the ceasefire was indefinite. The dispatch's argument was structural: the Duration Paradox from the first Fieldnote was no longer a forecast. It was the situation.

Forty-eight hours later, the situation has hardened. Today, on day sixty-two of the war:

Brent crude crossed $126 per barrel, a four-year high. The Pentagon told Congress the war has cost twenty-five billion dollars; Al Jazeera estimated the true figure at closer to one trillion when supply-chain effects, allied losses, and rebuilding obligations are included. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth refused, under congressional questioning, to estimate when the war might end. The 60-day deadline under the 1973 War Powers Resolution arrived with no congressional vote scheduled to authorize continued hostilities. The United Arab Emirates announced its departure from OPEC. Iran's "shadow fleet" was documented evading the U.S. naval blockade through ship-to-ship transfers and false flag registrations. Pakistan opened overland trade routes into Iran, bypassing the maritime blockade entirely. The U.S. Treasury warned that any vessel paying Iranian transit tolls could face secondary sanctions, including buyers of Iranian oil downstream. Lufthansa cut transcontinental flights as jet fuel supplies tightened. Trump told reporters the blockade could last "months."

The phrase "frozen conflict" entered mainstream coverage today, in an Al Jazeera analysis asking: Could the US-Iran war become a protracted "frozen" conflict? The phrase is borrowed from the post-Soviet space; from Transnistria, Abkhazia, Nagorno-Karabakh, the Donbas before 2022. It describes a war that does not end and does not resolve; that suspends itself in a state of unresolved hostility, with no peace treaty and no return to the prior order. The closest historical analog is the Korean War, which has been formally unresolved since 1953.

This dispatch is about what it means for a war to freeze. Specifically, it is about the four clocks now running simultaneously over the Iran war, the structural reasons each of them has slowed down, and what happens to a global system when none of its calendar mechanisms can deliver the resolution the situation requires.

Chapter I

The Four Clocks

The Dual Blockade dispatch identified two clocks running over the war: the speed at which economic damage accumulates, and the speed at which diplomatic resolution advances. The economic clock runs continuously. The diplomatic clock advances in discrete rounds separated by weeks. The gap between them is where the global economy absorbs the cost.

In the past forty-eight hours, two more clocks have become operative, and both have slowed.

The military clock. The U.S. Navy's primary carrier in the region, the USS Gerald R. Ford, is set to depart the Persian Gulf within days, according to CBS News reporting. CENTCOM commander General Erik Kurilla is briefing the president on a new set of military options, but Hegseth's congressional testimony suggested the Pentagon does not have a plan to break the stalemate through escalation; he repeatedly declined to specify what additional military action the United States is prepared to take, and he refused to characterize the conflict as a "quagmire" while also refusing to provide an estimated end date. The military clock has slowed because the available military options have been exhausted without producing political resolution. The bombing campaign succeeded militarily and failed politically. There is no second campaign that improves on this outcome at acceptable cost.

The constitutional clock. Today is the 60-day mark under the War Powers Resolution of 1973, which requires the president to terminate U.S. armed forces deployed in hostilities within 60 days unless Congress has declared war or specifically authorized continued action. No such authorization exists. No congressional vote has been scheduled. Senate Republicans blocked an attempt to force a related war powers vote on Cuba two days ago, signaling that the leadership has no appetite for forcing the Iran question. The president is, by the plain text of the statute, in violation of the Resolution; or, as has happened consistently since 1973, the Resolution will simply be ignored, joining the Korean War, the Vietnam escalation, the Lebanon deployment, the Libya intervention, and a dozen other conflicts that proceeded past their statutory deadlines without legal consequence. The constitutional clock has slowed because the institution responsible for advancing it has chosen not to.

That makes four clocks running simultaneously over the war. The economic clock runs continuously and accelerates as oil prices rise. The diplomatic clock advances in rounds separated by weeks. The military clock has slowed to a near-stop because escalation no longer offers a path to resolution. The constitutional clock has slowed because Congress has chosen not to advance it. None of the four is moving fast enough to end the war on a timeline the global economy can absorb without structural damage.

Four clocks. None of them ticking at the speed the war requires. The economic damage accumulates daily. The diplomatic process advances in rounds. The military options are exhausted. The constitutional mechanism is dormant. This is what a frozen conflict looks like; not a war that ends, but a war that runs out of clocks. — The temporal architecture of the freeze
Chapter II

The Reorganization

The first dispatch in this series, written on the war's eighth day, predicted that a long war would not be a longer version of a short war. It would be a structural reorganization. "A short war is a shock. A long war is a condition. The line between them is the line between a system that recovers and a system that reorganizes around the damage."

The reorganization is now visible across every domain the global economy depends on, and it is happening at a speed that would have been impossible without the war's pressure to accelerate it.

The cartel reorganization. The United Arab Emirates announced on April 28 that it would withdraw from the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries. The UAE has been an OPEC member since 1967. Its departure ends a pattern of cartel discipline that has structured global oil markets for nearly six decades. The Guardian reported that the move "weakened" OPEC and was being viewed in Washington as a strategic win. CNBC asked the question that matters: who could be next? Saudi Arabia is publicly committed to the cartel, but the war has demonstrated that bilateral arrangements with the United States now offer better insurance against regional instability than collective bargaining through a Tehran-influenced organization. The structure that has set global oil prices since the 1970s lost a member during the dispatch's analytical window, and the reasons it lost a member are structural, not transactional.

The shipping reorganization. Iran's shadow fleet, documented today by Al Jazeera, is running parallel infrastructure under flag-of-convenience registrations; predominantly Pakistani, with some Russian-flagged vessels. The fleet uses ship-to-ship transfers in international waters to disguise origin, false transponder data to spoof location, and informal payment networks denominated in yuan and cryptocurrency. This is the same infrastructure Russia developed after 2022 to evade Western sanctions on its oil exports. The infrastructure exists. It is operational. It is being expanded by the war, and it is now serving two of the world's three sanctioned major energy exporters; with Venezuela likely to join the system if the United States expands enforcement.

The overland reorganization. Pakistan opened road trade routes into Iran today, bypassing the maritime blockade entirely. Goods that previously moved through the Strait of Hormuz are now moving overland through Balochistan. The volumes are small relative to maritime trade, but the precedent matters: when the maritime chokepoint becomes contested, the overland alternative becomes economically viable, and the political relationships required to maintain it solidify. Pakistan is now structurally tied to Iran's economic survival in a way it was not three months ago.

The energy-transition reorganization. The UN climate chief noted today that the Iran war has fueled a "boom" in renewable power adoption. BBC Business reported that homeowners in the United Kingdom are installing solar panels at the highest rate ever recorded as oil prices surge. A coalition of states gathered in Colombia to announce a coordinated push to "ditch coal, oil and gas," timed to coincide with the energy crisis. The transition that was supposed to happen over decades is being compressed by the war into months; not by climate policy but by price signal. Every household, business, and utility evaluating its energy mix is now doing so against the backdrop of $126-per-barrel oil and the explicit possibility of months more of disruption.

◆ The reorganization the dispatch did not anticipate

The Dual Blockade dispatch identified the dual structure of the blockade itself. It did not anticipate the speed at which the system would reorganize around the blockade. OPEC losing a member, the shadow fleet operationalizing across two sanctioned exporters, Pakistan establishing overland trade with Iran, and the energy transition compressing from decades to months; all of these are happening now, simultaneously, because the blockade has created the conditions in which they become economically rational. The system is not waiting for the war to end. It is reorganizing around the assumption that the war will not end.

Chapter III

The Leakage Problem

A blockade is a leak-control mechanism. It works by making the cost of evading it higher than the cost of complying with it. When the cost of evasion drops below the cost of compliance, the blockade leaks; and a leaky blockade is, structurally, a different instrument than a tight one.

The U.S. naval blockade of Iran is leaking on three vectors simultaneously, and the leaks are widening rather than narrowing.

The first leak is the shadow fleet. Iran has documented capacity to move at least several hundred thousand barrels per day through ship-to-ship transfers and reflagged vessels, with the cargo arriving at destination ports as nominally legitimate Russian or other-origin oil. The U.S. Navy boards and seizes vessels it can identify as Iranian-controlled, but identification is the binding constraint. The shadow fleet operates in the gap between what the Navy can intercept and what it can verify, and the gap is structurally larger than the Navy's interdiction capacity.

The second leak is the overland route. Pakistan's land border with Iran is not blockaded and cannot practically be blockaded. Goods moving overland from Iran into Pakistan, and from Pakistan onward into Central Asia and China, do not pass through the Strait of Hormuz at any point. The volumes are growing. The infrastructure is being upgraded. Each truck convoy that successfully crosses the border establishes the route as a viable alternative, and the routes that work get used more.

The third leak is the toll-payment system. The U.S. Treasury today warned that vessels paying Iranian transit tolls could face secondary sanctions; a warning that confirms what was already true: the toll system is functioning, payments are being made, and the United States has been unable to stop them. Treasury's announcement is a demand-side intervention because the supply-side intervention (the naval blockade) has not stopped the payments. Whether the secondary-sanctions threat will work is unclear, but the fact that it is being deployed indicates that the blockade itself is not closing the leak.

This is the temporal-mismatch thesis applied to enforcement. The U.S. capacity to intercept, identify, and prosecute violations advances at the speed of military and legal procedure. The capacity of the system being blockaded to develop workarounds advances at the speed of economic adaptation. The two speeds are not commensurable. Every week the blockade continues, the workarounds become more sophisticated and more institutionalized, and the marginal cost of a new leak decreases. The blockade does not become tighter over time. It becomes more porous.

A tight blockade fails the day it leaks. A leaky blockade fails over months, as the leaks become institutionalized and the workarounds become permanent. The U.S. blockade of Iran has been leaking for at least four weeks. Each week, the leaks are wider than the week before, and the costs of the leaks are absorbed not by the blockading party but by the global economy that depends on the system the blockade is supposed to protect. — The leakage dynamic
Chapter IV

The Pricing Gap

CNBC reported today that "markets are still underpricing Iran war risks." The S&P 500 has rallied since the ceasefire was announced on April 8, on the assumption that resolution is imminent and the strait will reopen. Brent crude is at $126 a barrel, but equity markets are trading as if the disruption is temporary. Niall Ferguson, writing in The Free Press on April 20, made the same observation in different terms; the gap between what President Trump declares on Truth Social ("THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ IS COMPLETELY OPEN AND READY FOR BUSINESS") and the operational reality (vessels turning back from the strait under Iranian threat) is being mirrored in financial markets that are pricing the Truth Social version rather than the operational reality.

The Amplification Channels dispatch, published two weeks ago, warned that the IMF had identified five amplification channels through which a second shock could cascade into systemic crisis. Two weeks later, the conditions for that cascade have intensified.

The Bank of England held rates at 3.75% today and signaled that further increases may be required as the Iran war fuels inflation. The European Central Bank held its rates as well. Eurozone inflation rose to 3.0%, breaching the ECB's target. Germany's economy posted surprise growth, but the country's cabinet is now discussing whether the 2027 budget can absorb continued energy costs at current levels. The Bank of Japan continues to hold rates near zero, but yen weakness is approaching levels that triggered intervention in 2024. Every major central bank is now balancing inflation pressure from the war against growth concerns from the same source, and the policy space described in the IMF's April Global Financial Stability Report continues to compress.

The pricing gap is the second-order temporal mismatch. Markets price expected outcomes; they discount the value of an asset based on the probability-weighted distribution of future cash flows. When the actual situation diverges from market expectations, prices adjust. But the adjustment happens at the speed at which the divergence becomes undeniable; not at the speed at which the divergence is occurring. Markets can hold a wrong price for weeks before reality forces correction, and the correction, when it comes, is rarely smooth.

Niall Ferguson's analogy to July 1914 is instructive. The London bond market did not price the risk of world war until the war was already underway; the Rothschilds, supposedly the best-informed investors in the world, were caught flat-footed. The 1914 case is a paradigmatic example of a market holding a stable price across a window in which the underlying conditions had become unstable. The current oil-price environment, with Brent at $126 but equities still trading near pre-war highs, is the same structural pattern. The market is pricing the resolution that has not occurred. The reality is the leakage, the reorganization, and the freeze.

Chapter V

The Constitutional Silence

The 1973 War Powers Resolution requires the president to "terminate any use of United States Armed Forces" in hostilities within 60 days unless Congress has authorized continued action through a declaration of war or specific statutory authorization. Today is the 60-day mark for the Iran war. Neither authorization exists. Neither has been seriously proposed.

This is not new. The Resolution has been substantially ignored by every administration since it was passed over President Nixon's veto in November 1973. The Korean War proceeded without a declaration. Vietnam did. Lebanon, Grenada, Panama, Somalia, the Balkans, Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya, Syria; the long list of post-1973 American military operations conducted past the 60-day deadline without specific authorization has produced no judicial enforcement and no congressional consequence. The Resolution is, in practice, a statute that is observed only when the president chooses to observe it.

What is new in the current situation is not the silence. It is the visibility of the silence. The Iran war is the largest American military operation since the 2003 Iraq invasion. It is being fought in a domain (energy markets) that affects every household. It is producing effects (inflation, jet fuel shortages, recession warnings, OPEC fragmentation) that are being attributed correctly by mainstream media to the war's continued duration. And the constitutional mechanism designed to force a congressional decision about whether to continue is being treated as if it does not exist.

This is the temporal-mismatch thesis applied to constitutional governance. The institution responsible for authorizing or terminating the war (Congress) is operating at a tempo that is no longer compatible with the tempo of the events it is supposed to govern. The 60-day clock was designed in 1973 for an era in which a major American military operation could plausibly be expected to reach a decisive military outcome within two months. The Iran war demonstrates that this assumption no longer holds. The military phase ended weeks ago. The blockade phase is open-ended. The economic phase will continue for as long as the blockade does. The constitutional mechanism has nothing to act on, because there is no decisive moment for it to act on; there is only an ongoing condition.

◆ The institution that did not show up

The Liar's Dividend described an epistemic foundation eroding faster than verification could re-establish it. The Frozen Conflict describes a constitutional foundation eroding faster than statutory enforcement can re-establish it. In both cases, the institutional speed is fundamentally incompatible with the speed of the conditions the institution was designed to address. The 60-day clock was a 1973 answer to a 1973 problem. The 2026 problem is structurally different, and the institution has not adapted to address it.

Chapter VI

What This Confirms

This is the second sequel in the series. The Dual Blockade returned to Dispatch №01's domain to show the Duration Paradox confirmed. The Frozen Conflict returns to the same domain to show the next phase: the system reorganizing around the damage that the Duration Paradox produced.

The first dispatch argued that the war's decisive variable would be duration. Two months later, duration is not just the variable. It is the entire war. There is no battlefield to win or lose. There is only the question of who can endure the economic stalemate longer; and the war has now become structurally indistinguishable from the post-Soviet frozen conflicts that taught the world what it looks like when wars don't end.

The Toll Booth and the Tripwire predicted that the blockade would fracture the alliance. The fracture is now structural. The U.K. ambassador's leaked remarks describing the U.S.-U.K. relationship as "no longer special" except for the Israel link, the European Article 42.7 mutual-defense clause being publicly discussed as a NATO substitute, the Britain-led nine-nation joint naval force being established as a "complement" to NATO; these are not crisis-management measures. They are the foundations of an alliance system being constructed in parallel with the existing one, on the assumption that the existing one has weakened beyond reliable use.

The Amplification Channels predicted that the financial system's buffers were being drawn down. The buffers are now being deployed. Central banks are holding rates at restrictive levels because they cannot cut without reigniting inflation. Government debt-to-GDP ratios are at historic highs. The IMF's policy-space warning has aged into the operational constraint shaping every central-bank meeting and every fiscal-budget discussion in the major economies.

The 280-Character Cabinet predicted that state communication would diverge from state policy. The divergence is now central to the war's information architecture. Trump's Truth Social declarations that the strait is "completely open and ready for business" are pricing equities at one level while operational reality (vessels turning back, Iranian shadow fleet evading interdiction, oil at $126) is pricing commodities at another level. The president's communication operates at platform speed; the strait operates at maritime speed; the markets are caught between them.

And the Liar's Dividend predicted that epistemic erosion would weaken the foundation of democratic decision-making. Hegseth's congressional testimony today, in which he refused to characterize the war's duration, refused to estimate when it would end, refused to call it a quagmire, and dismissed critics rather than engaging with their substantive concerns, is the institutional version of the same erosion. The institution responsible for oversight (Congress) cannot get a straight answer about the operation it is supposed to oversee, and the institutional mechanism designed to force an answer (the War Powers Resolution) has been allowed to lapse into dormancy.

Five dispatches in the original series. All five have been confirmed by events of the past two months, in the specific structural form they predicted. The thesis is not just durable. It is operational; visible in real time, across multiple domains, in the actions of named institutions and named officials, on a clock the publication has been running since the war began.

Chapter VII

What a Frozen Conflict Looks Like

The post-Soviet frozen conflicts (Transnistria, Abkhazia, South Ossetia, Nagorno-Karabakh before 2020) all share a set of structural features. The military phase ends without a peace treaty. The political question that drove the war remains unresolved. A de facto line of control hardens into a de facto border. Economic relationships reorient around the line; trade routes shift, financial flows reroute, allegiances solidify. The international community proposes resolutions that no one signs. The participants develop institutional infrastructure for managing the unresolved condition; checkpoints, customs arrangements, parallel currencies, dual-use legal frameworks. The conflict becomes the new normal, and the new normal becomes durable in ways the participants did not anticipate when the freezing began.

The Iran war is exhibiting all of these features simultaneously, on a compressed timeline.

The military phase ended without a peace treaty. The political question (Iran's nuclear program, Iran's regional proxies, Iran's relationship to the global financial system) remains unresolved. A de facto line of control has hardened around the Strait of Hormuz; Iran controls one side of the blockade, the United States controls the other, and the line between them is the strait itself. Economic relationships are reorienting; UAE leaving OPEC, Pakistan opening overland routes, the shadow fleet expanding, the energy transition accelerating. The international community has proposed resolutions (the Islamabad talks, Pakistani mediation, the Iranian strait-for-blockade offer) that no one has signed. And the participants are developing institutional infrastructure for managing the unresolved condition; the Iranian toll-payment system, the U.S. Treasury secondary-sanctions framework, the European Article 42.7 mutual-defense discussions, the British nine-nation naval force.

The Korean War analogy is the closest historical parallel. Active hostilities ended in July 1953. The political question (the legitimacy of the two governments) was never resolved. A de facto border (the 38th parallel, hardened into the DMZ) became the most heavily fortified frontier in the world. Economic relationships reoriented; South Korea integrated with the United States and Japan, North Korea with China and the Soviet Union. The institutional infrastructure of the freeze (the Korean Armistice Agreement, the Joint Security Area, the United Nations Command) became durable. The war has now been frozen for seventy-three years, and there is no scenario in which it ends in 2026.

The Iran war's freeze is two months old, not seventy-three years. But the structural features that produce frozen conflicts are present. The institutional features that distinguish a frozen conflict from a temporary stalemate are appearing. And the economic and political incentives that lock frozen conflicts in place are now operative on multiple sides simultaneously.

Frozen conflicts do not end because someone wins. They end when the institutional infrastructure built to manage the freeze becomes more costly to maintain than the political compromise required to dissolve it. That calculus, on the Korean Peninsula, has not yet been completed; seventy-three years in. There is no reason to expect it to be completed quickly on the Strait of Hormuz. — The durability of the freeze

Day sixty-two. Four clocks. None of them ticking at the speed the war requires. The system is reorganizing around the damage. The damage is becoming the system. That is what a frozen conflict looks like.

◆ End of dispatch

Postscript

This is the second sequel in the series, and it makes a structural point that the first sequel only implied.

The temporal-mismatch thesis is not a one-time observation about a single moment. It is a description of how complex systems behave under sustained pressure. The Dual Blockade dispatch showed the pattern compounding within a single domain over time. The Frozen Conflict shows the pattern producing a new structural condition; one in which the system has reorganized around the damage to such an extent that the damage is no longer an interruption to be repaired. It is the new operating environment.

This is the most consequential version of the thesis the series has produced. Institutional slowness, institutional dismantling, epistemic erosion, the personal gap; the original four variations each described a system failing to respond to a threat. The frozen conflict describes what comes after the failure has persisted long enough to become permanent. The threat does not get addressed. It gets institutionalized. The workarounds become the architecture. The exceptions become the rules. The system that emerges from the freeze is not the system that entered it, and the system that entered it cannot be restored without dismantling the architecture that has been built to manage its absence.

Five dispatches predicted the conditions of this moment. Two months of events have confirmed them. The dispatches will continue to track the freeze as it deepens, because the freeze is the condition that the next several years of global politics, economics, and institutional design will be conducted under. The war is no longer the news. The war is the calendar.
