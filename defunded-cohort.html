A dispatch in seven movements

The Defunded
Cohort

On April 1, the president said the federal government could not afford day care while fighting a war. The statement was an admission. The structural meaning of the admission is the dispatch.

Published May 5, 2026
Reading time ~26 minutes
Filed under Economics / Policy
Companion to №10, №13, №14

On Wednesday, April 1, 2026, President Trump hosted a private Easter luncheon at the White House. The event was closed to the press. The White House inadvertently posted video of the president's remarks to its official YouTube channel, then took the video down when staff recognized what he had said. The video had already been captured. The clip is now in wide circulation.

Addressing Office of Management and Budget Director Russell Vought, the president said: "I said to Russell, 'Don't send any money for day care, because the United States can't take care of day care.' That has to be up to a state. We can't take care of day care. We're a big country. We have 50 states. We have all these other people. We're fighting wars. We can't take care of day care."

He continued: "It's not possible for us to take care of day care, Medicaid, Medicare, all these individual things. They can do it on a state basis. You can't do it on a federal. We have to take care of one thing: military protection."

The dispatch is not about whether the president meant what he said. The remarks were extemporaneous, the White House subsequently issued a clarifying statement (the president was referring to "rooting out fraud," spokeswoman Olivia Wales said), and OMB Director Vought, when grilled by the House Budget Committee on April 15, denied that the administration intended to turn Medicare over to the states. The political defense of the statement is a separate question from its structural meaning, and the structural meaning is the only question Fieldnotes is equipped to answer.

The structural meaning is this: the federal government, in the administration's own framing, has acknowledged a binding trade-off between funding the war in Iran and funding the social infrastructure that the next decade of AI-driven labor disruption will require. The trade-off is not implicit in the budget. It was stated publicly, by the president, on Easter, at a luncheon. The FY2027 budget request that Vought submitted to Congress two weeks later is the operational version of the same trade-off, expressed in dollars rather than rhetoric.

The cohort that bears the cost of the trade-off is the cohort that the Reshaping Tsunami dispatch (Fieldnote №13) identified three days ago: the 6.1 million American workers in the high-exposure, low-adaptability pocket, with women disproportionately represented and clerical/administrative roles concentrated in midsized markets. The policy response that might have absorbed this cohort's transition is the response that has just been defunded.

Chapter I

The Admission

Begin with the rhetorical fact, because it is unusual.

Heads of state do not, as a rule, publicly state that their government cannot afford its commitments to its citizens because it has chosen to spend the money on a war. The reason is not that such trade-offs do not occur (they routinely do) but that the political cost of stating them plainly is normally prohibitive. The Cold War was financed in part by deferring civilian investment. The Iraq and Afghanistan wars were financed by debt that future generations would inherit. The 2008 banking sector rescue was financed by suppressing subsequent fiscal capacity. Each of these trade-offs was real. None of them was announced as a trade-off. The accounting was always partial, the framing was always optimistic, and the decision was always presented as a choice that did not require giving up anything that mattered.

Trump's April 1 statement was different. The trade-off was named, on the record, in the simplest possible terms: we cannot fund day care, Medicaid, and Medicare because we are fighting wars. The list of things being given up was specific. The reason they were being given up was specific. The hierarchy of priorities was specific: "We have to take care of one thing: military protection."

The White House subsequently described the comments as having been taken out of context, with the spokeswoman arguing that the president was referring to fraud in the named programs rather than to their fundamental funding. The argument is implausible on its face; the president did not mention fraud in the relevant passage, and the reasoning he offered (the federal government is fighting wars, the country is large, the fifty states should bear the responsibility) does not relate to fraud at all. But the question of whether the White House's after-the-fact reframing is honest is less interesting than the question of why the reframing was needed. The reframing was needed because the original statement plainly described a trade-off that the administration's budget operationalizes but that no administration is supposed to acknowledge in those terms.

Two weeks after the luncheon, on April 15, OMB Director Russell Vought defended the FY2027 budget before the House Budget Committee. The budget proposes $1.5 trillion in total defense spending; a 44% increase over current levels and a $442 billion increase in dollar terms. It cuts nondefense discretionary spending by 10% across the board. The cuts include reductions to Medicaid, housing assistance, childcare, and home energy aid for low-income seniors. Vought, asked directly about the president's Easter remarks, sidestepped: he claimed the president had been "talking about fraud." Representative Brendan Boyle pointed out that the president had not mentioned fraud. The exchange did not produce a clarification. It produced a careful avoidance of clarification.

The president stated the trade-off plainly. The budget operationalizes it. The administration's after-the-fact reframing of the statement as being "about fraud" is the political layer that exists because the structural admission is too significant to be allowed to stand without modification. The reframing is the proof that the original was not a misstatement but a description. — The unusual rhetorical fact
Chapter II

The Numbers

The trade-off is verifiable in the budget arithmetic. The numbers are public, sourced to the FY2027 budget request submitted by OMB to Congress on April 13 and defended by Vought before the House Budget Committee on April 15.

Defense. The proposed defense top line is $1.5 trillion, up from a current $1.04 trillion. The increase, $442 billion, is approximately 44 percent. By comparison, the FY2024 defense budget under the Biden administration was $886 billion. The proposed FY2027 figure is roughly 70 percent higher than the last pre-war defense top line.

Iran war supplemental. The administration is reportedly seeking an additional $200 billion supplemental appropriation outside the regular budget process to fund the ongoing Iran operations and immigration enforcement. The Pentagon has acknowledged $25 billion in war costs through April 29. Independent estimates from Al Jazeera and other outlets suggest the actual cost, when supply-chain effects and allied losses are included, is closer to $1 trillion. The supplemental request, if approved, would bring the total Iran-related defense expenditure into territory comparable to the early phases of the Iraq War.

Nondefense discretionary cuts. The 10 percent across-the-board cut to nondefense discretionary spending falls on programs that support the cohort the Reshaping Tsunami identified: Medicaid (which already absorbed $911 billion in cuts under the One Big Beautiful Bill enacted last year, projected by CBO to strip 15 to 17 million Americans of healthcare coverage), the Child Care and Development Block Grant, Temporary Assistance for Needy Families, housing assistance, home energy aid, and the WIC nutrition program. Representative Morgan McGarvey, examining page 164 of the budget document during Vought's testimony, identified a provision slashing the WIC fruit and vegetable benefit for breastfeeding mothers from $52 to $13 per month.

Healthcare infrastructure. A Public Citizen study published in mid-April found that nearly 450 hospitals nationwide could close, slash services, or lay off workers due to the Medicaid cuts in the One Big Beautiful Bill. The study identified roughly 90 hospitals at risk in the Northeast alone, ranging from urban areas like New York City to rural regions across the Midwest and Mountain West.

National debt. The national debt stands near $39 trillion. CBO has determined that the One Big Beautiful Bill, enacted in 2025, adds more to the deficit than any single piece of legislation in American history. The proposed FY2027 budget would add further to that deficit. Representative Pramila Jayapal noted during Vought's testimony that the Department of Defense has now failed eight consecutive audits and remains the only federal agency that has never passed one.

◆ The arithmetic of the trade-off

$442 billion proposed increase to defense. $911 billion in Medicaid cuts already enacted. $200 billion supplemental requested for Iran operations. 15 to 17 million Americans projected to lose healthcare coverage. 450 hospitals at risk of closure. The numbers are not estimates from critics. They are figures from the administration's own budget submissions and from the CBO's nonpartisan scoring. The trade-off the president described at the Easter luncheon is the trade-off the budget operationalizes. The political fight is over how to describe it, not over whether it exists.

Chapter III

The Cohort

The Reshaping Tsunami dispatch, published three days ago, identified a specific population whose policy bridge is most needed and least available. It is worth restating the demographic specifics, because they are the population the April 1 statement and the FY2027 budget have explicitly defunded.

Brookings' January 2026 study identified 6.1 million American workers (roughly 4.2 percent of the workforce) sitting in the high-exposure, low-adaptability pocket of the labor market. The demographic profile is stark.

86 percent are women. They are concentrated in clerical and administrative roles: 2.5 million office clerks, 1.7 million secretaries and administrative assistants, hundreds of thousands of bookkeeping clerks and customer-service representatives. They are mostly in their forties and fifties, with savings inadequate for an unfunded transition period and care responsibilities that make geographic relocation impractical. They are concentrated in midsized markets (university towns, smaller metropolitan areas, the Mountain West and Midwest), where alternative employment is geographically scarce.

This is the population for which the social infrastructure being defunded was built. Medicaid covers 84.5 million Americans at a cost of approximately $932 billion. Medicare covers 66 million Americans at a cost of approximately $1.1 trillion. The Child Care and Development Block Grant subsidizes care for working parents whose income falls below 85 percent of the state median. WIC provides nutrition support for low-income women, infants, and children. None of these programs is generous in international terms. Each is the floor below which a working family cannot reasonably be expected to function. The 6.1-million-person Reshaping Tsunami cohort is overwhelmingly dependent on this floor; not because they are poor (most are not, in present terms) but because their adaptive capacity to absorb a labor-market shock has been undercut by decades of stagnant wages, eroded benefits, and the rising cost of housing, healthcare, and child care.

The arithmetic of the situation is this. The cohort needs three things if it is to navigate the AI-driven role redesign without falling out of the labor market entirely: time to retrain (which requires income support during the transition), affordable healthcare (because the existing employer-provided insurance is contingent on continuous employment), and child care (because most of the cohort has dependents and most of the cohort cannot retrain while also providing 50 hours per week of unpaid care). The April 1 statement and the FY2027 budget reduce or eliminate the federal contribution to all three. The states, the president argued, should pick them up. Most states cannot.

This is not a distributional question of the kind that political debate normally engages. It is a structural question. A cohort being asked to absorb a once-in-a-generation transformation of work is being asked to do so without the social infrastructure that has historically been the precondition for absorbing transformations of that magnitude. The 1930s New Deal absorbed mass industrial unemployment with public-works employment, social insurance, and labor-rights infrastructure. The post-1945 settlement absorbed the demobilization of millions of veterans with the GI Bill, mass higher-education funding, and federally backed mortgage finance. The 1960s Great Society absorbed the entry of the baby boom into the labor market with Medicare, Medicaid, and the expansion of higher education. Each transformation produced a structural response. The current transformation is being met with a structural withdrawal.

The 6.1 million workers in the Reshaping Tsunami cohort are not asking for transfers. They are working. They will continue working as long as their roles continue to exist in recognizable form. The question is what they do when the roles are remade beyond their adaptive capacity, and the answer offered by the April 1 statement is that they should ask their state government, which will not have the resources to answer either. — The structural withdrawal
Chapter IV

The State Devolution

The president's proposed solution, articulated in the April 1 remarks, was that state governments should fund the programs the federal government would no longer fund. The states, he said, "have to raise their taxes" to cover the costs, and the federal government "could lower our taxes a little bit to them to make up" for it. The framing is plausible at the level of political rhetoric. It is implausible at the level of fiscal arithmetic.

State governments, with rare exceptions, are constitutionally required to balance their budgets. Forty-nine of the fifty states have such requirements. The federal government has no such requirement, which is precisely why federal fiscal capacity has historically absorbed the cyclical and structural shocks that state governments cannot. When unemployment rises, federal Medicaid spending rises with it; states cannot deficit-finance the response. When a recession hits, federal income support and unemployment insurance expand; states must cut services and raise taxes at the worst possible moment.

The proposed devolution would shift this counter-cyclical responsibility from the level of government that can absorb it to the level of government that cannot. A state experiencing rising unemployment and falling tax revenues would be asked, simultaneously, to expand its Medicaid program (because more residents qualify) and to fund a child-care program the federal government previously subsidized. The state would be required to do this while balancing its budget. The only way to do that is by cutting other services or raising taxes during a downturn; the textbook policy error that federal fiscal capacity exists to prevent.

The geographic distribution of the Reshaping Tsunami cohort makes this concern more acute. The cohort is concentrated in midsized markets, which are concentrated in states with relatively limited fiscal capacity. The states with the largest concentrations of high-exposure clerical workers (Iowa, Indiana, Ohio, parts of Pennsylvania, the Mountain West) are not states with the wealth bases that California or New York could deploy to backstop a federal withdrawal. The proposed devolution would concentrate the cost of the federal withdrawal in the states least equipped to absorb it, which would in turn concentrate the human cost on the populations within those states with the least adaptive capacity.

This is not a hypothetical. The Public Citizen analysis of the One Big Beautiful Bill's Medicaid cuts found that hospital closures would be concentrated in rural and midsized-market areas across the same states that contain the highest concentrations of the high-exposure cohort. The cohort that Brookings identified as least able to navigate AI-driven labor disruption is the same cohort that is losing access to healthcare infrastructure as the Medicaid cuts ripple through the hospital sector. The state-devolution proposal would compound the pressure by removing the federal floor that has historically prevented these regions from facing simultaneous losses of healthcare access, income support, and child-care funding.

◆ The structural impossibility of state devolution at scale

The federal government did not absorb Medicaid, Medicare, child-care funding, and the rest of the social safety net because of a preference for centralization. It absorbed them because the states could not, on their own balance-sheet capacity, sustain counter-cyclical social spending during the periods when such spending is most necessary. The proposed devolution does not correct an inefficiency. It removes a counter-cyclical buffer at precisely the moment when the buffer is most needed; with AI-driven labor displacement accelerating, an active war consuming federal fiscal capacity, and the states experiencing their own revenue compression as the same dynamics work through their economies. The arithmetic does not work. The political rhetoric works regardless.

Chapter V

The Trade-Off Over Time

The April 1 statement is not the first time an American administration has chosen war spending over social spending. The pattern recurs across the post-1945 record, and the historical comparison is instructive about what is structurally distinct in the current case.

Lyndon Johnson's "guns and butter" doctrine in 1965 attempted to fund the Vietnam War and the Great Society simultaneously, financed by deficit spending and tax adjustments. The strategy partially succeeded for several years before producing the inflation that defined the late 1960s and the 1970s. Johnson did not announce a trade-off; he announced that there was no trade-off, that the country was wealthy enough to do both. The eventual fiscal pressure was real but came years after the political commitment had been made. The Great Society programs were funded; the Vietnam War was funded; the cost was deferred.

Ronald Reagan's defense buildup in the early 1980s was financed by deficit spending and by reductions in domestic programs, particularly through the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1981. Reagan did argue for a trade-off, but the trade-off he argued for was between government spending generally and economic dynamism, with the implicit claim that defense spending was different in kind from social spending because it generated technological externalities and ensured the conditions under which the economy could function. The framing was contestable but it was at least an argument; the social spending cuts were defended as means to a broader prosperity that would absorb the displaced.

The post-9/11 wars in Iraq and Afghanistan were financed almost entirely by debt. The Bush and Obama administrations, between 2001 and 2014, conducted what was effectively an off-budget war, with the Pentagon's regular appropriations supplemented by overseas contingency operations funding that did not require offsetting reductions elsewhere. The cost was passed to future generations rather than negotiated against current commitments. The trade-off existed, in a long-term fiscal sense, but it was not made visible in any year's budget.

The April 1 statement is structurally different from each of these cases. It is not Johnson's optimism. It is not Reagan's argument that defense spending is generative of broader prosperity. It is not the Bush-Obama deferral to the future. It is a plain statement that the federal government cannot fund the social infrastructure because it has chosen to fund the war, with the proposed solution being state-level devolution that the arithmetic does not support. The statement compresses what previous administrations distributed across rhetoric, deferral, and creative accounting. The trade-off is on the record. The cost is being assigned to a specific cohort. The structural meaning is not hidden by the framing.

This is what makes the statement worth dispatching about. Not the politics, which will resolve themselves through the standard mechanisms of political debate. But the structural admission, which sets a precedent for how the budget tensions of the next decade will be discussed. The next administration will inherit the precedent that war spending and social spending are explicitly traded off, that the trade-off can be stated publicly without producing political consequences sufficient to reverse it, and that the cohort whose social infrastructure is being defunded has no institutional voice loud enough to require attention. The precedent will outlast the administration that established it, in the same way the Terminated War's procedural innovation will outlast the war that produced it.

Chapter VI

What Rebuilding the Cohort Would Require

This dispatch is not a policy proposal, but it is worth being specific about what would be required to absorb the Reshaping Tsunami cohort's transition, because the specificity clarifies why the April 1 statement is structurally consequential.

A serious response to the AI-driven role-redesign problem would have at least four components. The sums attached are illustrative; they are derived from existing program costs and standard cost-benefit analyses, not from any single policy proposal.

One: extended income support during retraining. A program providing 18 to 24 months of partial income replacement (perhaps 50 to 60 percent of prior earnings, capped at the median) for workers in roles being substantially reshaped, conditioned on enrollment in approved training. Cost estimates for a program of this scale, covering 6 to 8 million workers over the next decade, range from $300 to $500 billion in cumulative federal expenditure.

Two: expanded portable healthcare. The Affordable Care Act subsidies, expanded under the COVID-era American Rescue Plan and made permanent under the Inflation Reduction Act, are the existing infrastructure for portable healthcare not tied to employment. Expanding subsidies to fully cover transitioning workers, and protecting the existing ACA Medicaid expansion from the One Big Beautiful Bill's cuts, would cost an estimated $50 to $100 billion annually over the relevant transition period.

Three: expanded child-care funding. The Child Care and Development Block Grant currently funds approximately $13 billion annually. Bringing federal child-care subsidies to the level recommended by the 2025 Bipartisan Policy Center analysis (capping costs at 7 to 10 percent of household income for families earning under 200 percent of the area median) would cost an additional $80 to $120 billion annually.

Four: regional adaptation funding. The Brookings cohort is concentrated in specific midsized markets that lack diversified local economies. Place-based investment in those regions; through a combination of infrastructure investment, business-formation support, and educational institution funding; would cost an estimated $50 billion annually for ten years.

The total: roughly $600 to $900 billion in additional annual federal spending for a decade, or $6 to $9 trillion cumulatively. This is, in scale, comparable to what the federal government has spent on counter-terrorism operations since 2001 (estimated at $8 to $10 trillion by the Costs of War Project at Brown University). It is roughly twice the cost of the One Big Beautiful Bill's tax cuts. It is roughly six to nine times the proposed FY2027 defense increase.

The numbers are large. They are also approximately what the federal government has, in fact, spent on previous transitions of comparable scale; the GI Bill, Medicare and Medicaid creation, the post-2008 financial crisis response. The current transition is not unfundable. It is unfunded by choice, and the choice has been made explicit by the April 1 statement and the FY2027 budget.

The cohort is not unaffordable. The infrastructure to support its transition is comparable in scale to what the federal government has spent on previous transitions of similar magnitude. The trade-off the April 1 statement names is not between the war and an impossibility. It is between the war and a set of policies that previous administrations of both parties have implemented at comparable cost. — The choice that has been made
Chapter VII

What This Confirms

This dispatch confirms what the previous fourteen Fieldnotes have been documenting, in the specific currency of fiscal commitment.

The 5,000-Day Gap argued that the policy response to AI-driven labor displacement was operating at legislative speed while the displacement operated at platform speed. The April 1 statement clarifies that the legislative response is not merely slow. It is being explicitly redirected, in real time, away from the cohort the displacement is reshaping and toward the war that the Frozen Conflict dispatch documented as a structural reorganization of the global economy. The temporal mismatch is not just a question of pace. It is a question of allocation, and the allocation has been made.

The Reshaping Tsunami identified the 6.1-million-person cohort whose adaptive capacity is the binding constraint on the labor market's ability to absorb AI-driven role redesign. The April 1 statement is the explicit defunding of the social infrastructure that would have provided that adaptive capacity. The cohort is not just exposed to a labor-market shock without sufficient support. It is exposed to the shock at the same moment that the existing support is being withdrawn.

The Frozen Conflict documented the war's expanding economic radius and the institutional dormancy that allowed it to continue without authorization. The April 1 statement is the fiscal expression of the same expansion: the war's costs are now being explicitly absorbed by reductions in social spending, with the trade-off named publicly and operationalized in the budget. The institutional silence the Frozen Conflict identified is the silence that allows this to occur without political resistance proportional to its scale.

The Terminated War, published yesterday, documented the procedural innovation by which the constitutional clock can be reset by executive declaration. The April 1 statement is the fiscal counterpart: the procedural innovation by which the budget allocation can be redirected from social spending to war spending while the political class describes the redirection as something other than what it is. Both innovations operate by reclassification. The first reclassifies hostilities into not-hostilities. The second reclassifies social-spending cuts into fraud-elimination measures. The structural mechanism is identical.

What this dispatch adds to the framework is the human ledger. The previous dispatches identified institutions, procedures, and patterns. This dispatch identifies the population that the institutions, procedures, and patterns are converging on. 6.1 million workers. Concentrated in clerical and administrative roles. 86 percent women. Mostly in their forties and fifties. Concentrated in midsized markets. Dependent on a federal social infrastructure that the FY2027 budget reduces, the April 1 statement explicitly defunds, and the Iran war absorbs the resources of. The cohort is not abstract. It is specifically identifiable, geographically located, and demographically described in the public research literature.

The dispatch is, in some ways, the publication's most direct intervention to date. It does not document a system failing structurally in the abstract. It documents a population whose support is being withdrawn at precisely the moment they will need it most, and it identifies the trade-off responsible for the withdrawal as one that the administration has stated publicly and operationalized in budget arithmetic. The cohort will absorb the cost. The cost will not be evenly distributed. The geographic concentration will produce regional collapses that will, in time, become individual collapses, household by household, in the midsized markets where the existing safety net was already strained.

"It's not possible for us to take care of day care, Medicaid, Medicare, all these individual things. They can do it on a state basis. We have to take care of one thing: military protection." The president stated the trade-off. The budget operationalized it. The cohort is the ledger.

◆ End of dispatch

Postscript

This is the fifteenth dispatch. The publication is now in its third month of operation, and the original ten-dispatch series has produced five sequels, each of which has identified an additional variation, refinement, or human consequence of the temporal-mismatch pattern.

The pattern's variations are accumulating: institutional slowness, institutional dismantling, epistemic erosion, the personal gap, continuous transformation, procedural redefinition. The Defunded Cohort does not add a new variation. It adds the human ledger that the previous variations imply but have not, until now, made specifically demographic.

The publication's analytical commitment has been to structural reading rather than partisan reading; to documenting patterns rather than advocating responses. This dispatch maintains that commitment. It does not argue that the FY2027 budget should be different. It argues that the budget reflects a trade-off that has been stated publicly, that the trade-off is between the war's continuation and the social infrastructure required to absorb a once-in-a-generation labor-market transformation, and that the cohort bearing the cost is identifiable in the existing research literature.

What the country does with this information is the question the next several years will answer. The question is not unanswerable. Previous administrations of both parties have funded transitions of comparable scale. The current administration has explicitly chosen not to. The choice is the dispatch. The cohort is the ledger. The next several years are the test of what the country does when the ledger comes due.
