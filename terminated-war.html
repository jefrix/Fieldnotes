◆ FIELDNOTES / POLITICS & CONSTITUTIONAL LAW №14 — MAY 2026
A dispatch in five movements

The Terminated
War

On May 1, the president informed Congress that hostilities with Iran had ended. Three days later, US helicopters sank six Iranian boats in the strait. Both statements are formally true. The gap between them is the new constitutional condition.

Published May 4, 2026
Reading time ~24 minutes
Filed under Politics / Constitutional Law
Companion to №06, №12

On Friday, May 1, 2026, President Trump sent letters to Speaker of the House Mike Johnson and Senator Chuck Grassley, the Senate's president pro tempore, formally notifying Congress that the war with Iran had ended. The letters were brief and procedurally precise: "On April 7, 2026, I ordered a two-week ceasefire. The ceasefire has since been extended. There has been no exchange of fire between United States forces and Iran since April 7, 2026. The hostilities that began on February 28, 2026, have terminated."

The letters were timed to the sixtieth day of the conflict, the statutory deadline under the 1973 War Powers Resolution at which a president must either obtain congressional authorization or terminate hostilities. The letter terminated them. By the procedural standards of the executive branch, the war was over.

Three days later, on Monday, May 4, US Apache and Seahawk helicopters destroyed six Iranian small boats in the Strait of Hormuz. Iranian missiles and drones were fired at US Navy ships, commercial vessels, and the United Arab Emirates. A South Korean tanker was struck and set on fire. Two US guided-missile destroyers sailed into the Persian Gulf as part of an operation called Project Freedom. President Trump posted on Truth Social that "we've shot down seven small Boats" and warned Iranian forces that they would be "blown off the face of the Earth" if they targeted American ships. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth and Admiral Brad Cooper held a press conference describing the operations as defensive escorts.

The dispatch is not about whether the war is "really" ongoing. By every observable metric, it is. The ceasefire that the May 1 letter referenced as evidence of termination is the same ceasefire under which the United States has maintained a naval blockade of Iranian ports since April 13, intercepted at least 49 commercial vessels, and now sunk seven Iranian boats. The ceasefire and the war are the same condition. They are described differently for different administrative purposes.

The dispatch is about the discovery, executed in 96 hours, that the War Powers Resolution can be neutralized by the simple expedient of declaring hostilities "terminated" while continuing to conduct them under different operational nomenclature. Project Freedom is not a war. It is a humanitarian shipping escort. The blockade is not a war. It is a maritime interdiction operation. The naval engagements are not a war. They are defensive measures consistent with the inherent right of self-defense. None of these is a hostility. The hostilities are terminated. The boats are nevertheless sunk.

This is a constitutional innovation. It will outlast the war. And it is, structurally, the next variation of the temporal-mismatch pattern this publication has been documenting for two months; one in which the institution responsible for advancing the constitutional clock is not merely dormant, as the Frozen Conflict dispatch described, but has been rendered procedurally inoperative by an executive innovation faster than the institution can adapt to recognize it.

Chapter I

The 96-Hour Termination

The timeline is worth laying out plainly, because the speed at which the legal fiction was assembled and tested is itself the dispatch's first observation.

Thursday, April 30. A senior administration official, briefing reporters anonymously ahead of the War Powers deadline, stated that "for War Powers Resolution purposes, the hostilities that began on Saturday, February 28, have terminated." This was the public preview of the legal argument. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth had previewed a related version of the argument before the Senate Armed Services Committee, suggesting that the 60-day clock can "pause or stop" during a ceasefire. Democratic senators rejected this view; the ongoing naval blockade, they argued, was itself a continuing act of war. Republican senators were more receptive.

Friday, May 1. The formal letters were transmitted to Speaker Mike Johnson and Senator Chuck Grassley. The phrasing was precise. The hostilities began on a date. They have terminated. The letter acknowledged that "the threat posed by Iran to the United States and our Armed Forces remains significant," but distinguished the threat (significant, ongoing) from the hostilities (terminated). The blockade was not mentioned. The continuing naval deployments were not mentioned. The 49 vessels that had been turned back from Iranian ports were not mentioned. The classification was specific: hostilities, defined narrowly, had terminated. Senator Richard Blumenthal of Connecticut responded on social media: "There's no pause button in the Constitution, or the War Powers Act. We're at war. We've been at war for 60 days. The blockade alone is a continuing act of war."

Saturday and Sunday, May 2 and 3. The administration prepared and announced Project Freedom. The framing was humanitarian. Hundreds of commercial vessels had been stranded in the Persian Gulf since the war began. The United States would "guide" them out, as President Trump put it on Truth Social, in an operation that involved guided-missile destroyers, over 100 land and sea-based aircraft, multi-domain unmanned platforms, and 15,000 service members. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent appeared on Fox News and declared that the United States now had "absolute control" of the Strait of Hormuz, dismissing Iran's navy as "a band of pirates."

Monday, May 4. The operational test arrived. Two US guided-missile destroyers entered the Persian Gulf as part of Project Freedom. Two US-flagged merchant vessels transited westward out of the strait. Iran fired cruise missiles, drones, and small boats at the convoy. US Apache and Seahawk helicopters sank six of the Iranian boats. Iranian missiles struck a South Korean-linked tanker. Iran fired drones at the United Arab Emirates. Trump posted that the destroyed boats numbered seven, not six, and warned that any Iranian forces targeting American ships would be "blown off the face of the Earth." Treasury Secretary Bessent's "absolute control" language from Sunday was, by Monday, a description of an active combat operation.

The hostilities began on a date. The hostilities have terminated. The boats nevertheless are sunk. None of these statements contradicts another within the administrative framework, and the framework is the new fact about how American military force will be conducted going forward. — The 96-hour timeline

The legal fiction held for ninety-six hours. What it produced in those ninety-six hours is the structural innovation this dispatch is about.

Chapter II

The Mechanism

The War Powers Resolution of 1973 was a specific institutional response to a specific institutional failure. Congress, having watched two presidents (Johnson and Nixon) conduct a major undeclared war in Vietnam without ever submitting it for legislative authorization, attempted to claw back its constitutional war-making authority by imposing a procedural constraint: the president must terminate any use of armed forces in hostilities within 60 days unless Congress has affirmatively authorized continued action.

The Resolution has been, in operational practice, a dead letter for most of its existence. Every president since Nixon has found ways to conduct military operations past the 60-day deadline without compliance. The mechanisms have varied. President Reagan deployed forces to Lebanon in 1982 and the deployment proceeded for over a year before Congress eventually passed authorization in October 1983. President Clinton conducted the 1999 Kosovo air campaign for 78 days without authorization, arguing that the bombing did not constitute "hostilities" within the meaning of the statute. President Obama conducted air operations against Libya in 2011 past the 60-day deadline, with the Office of Legal Counsel arguing that the operations did not meet the statutory definition of hostilities because no American troops were on the ground and the risk to American personnel was minimal. Each of these maneuvers narrowed the meaning of "hostilities" or extended the meaning of "authorization" or relied on congressional inaction to establish acquiescence.

What the Trump administration did on May 1 is procedurally distinct from these previous evasions. The earlier mechanisms held that the operation, ongoing, did not constitute hostilities within the meaning of the statute. The May 1 letter holds that the hostilities that did exist have terminated, and that subsequent operations under different operational designations are therefore not the same hostilities and not subject to the same statutory clock.

This is a categorically different argument. It does not redefine "hostilities." It accepts that hostilities existed, declares that they ended on a specific date (April 7, when the ceasefire was announced), and treats the subsequent operations as a separate matter. The blockade, established April 13, is not part of the terminated hostilities; it is a distinct operation conducted in the post-hostilities period. Project Freedom, established May 3 and tested May 4, is not part of the terminated hostilities; it is a humanitarian operation conducted in the post-hostilities period. The naval engagements, the destroyed boats, the missile and drone exchanges, the strike on the South Korean tanker; all of these are not part of the terminated hostilities. They are responses to threats that arose in the post-hostilities period.

The argument has the structural elegance of a category mistake. The hostilities, in the administration's framework, are a discrete, dateable phenomenon that began on February 28 and ended on April 7. The combat operations occurring on May 4 are something else. They occupy the same geographic space, involve the same forces, target the same adversary, and produce the same effects, but they are administratively classified differently. The category itself, having been declared closed, can no longer be used to challenge ongoing operations that fall outside it.

◆ The administrative-redefinition mechanism

Previous presidents narrowed the definition of "hostilities" to argue that ongoing operations did not trigger the statutory clock. The Trump administration accepted that the operations were hostilities, declared them terminated, and reclassified subsequent operations as something else. The first move negotiates with the statute. The second move dissolves it. By accepting the category and then closing it, the administration has demonstrated that the War Powers Resolution can be neutralized by simply asserting that the war it would constrain is no longer a war, even as the war continues.

Chapter III

The Backwards Reading

Søren Kierkegaard, in a journal entry from 1843, observed that "life can only be understood backwards, but it must be lived forwards." The line has been quoted often enough to have lost most of its sting, but the philosopher Slavoj Žižek, writing last week about Carlo Rovelli's forthcoming book on quantum mechanics and Kierkegaardian theology, returned to it in a way that is unexpectedly relevant to the May 1 letter.

Rovelli's argument, as Žižek summarizes it, is that quantum mechanics and Kierkegaardian existentialism share a structural insight: truth is intrinsically perspectival, and the meaning of an event is not fixed in advance but emerges through the choices made by observers in the event's unfolding. We do not know in advance what reality will emerge from the plurality of relational influences that constitute the next moment. We act under uncertainty, and the meaning of our actions is determined retrospectively, by what they turn out to have been.

This is a profound observation about the human condition. It is also, when deployed by an administration as a procedural device, a different thing entirely.

The May 1 letter is a backwards reading. It declares, retrospectively, that the war ended on April 7. The ceasefire that was announced on that date is interpreted, three weeks later, as the terminating event of a discrete period of hostilities. The blockade established on April 13, six days after the supposed termination, becomes a separate operation in a separate period. Monday's combat is read backwards through Friday's letter as a non-hostility, an engagement outside the hostilities that have been declared terminated. The administration is understanding the war backwards in order to live the next phase of it forwards without constitutional friction.

The Kierkegaardian observation, in its original setting, was descriptive. We cannot know in advance what our actions will mean; we must commit to them anyway. The administrative deployment of the same logic is prescriptive. We have committed to actions whose meaning the statute would have constrained; we therefore retrospectively reclassify them as having a different meaning. The constraint that would have applied to the action we took becomes inapplicable to the action we now declare we took.

This is not philosophy. It is a procedural exploitation of the structural fact that meaning is partially constituted by classification, and classification is, in administrative law, controlled by the classifier. The president classifies hostilities. The president has declared the hostilities terminated. The combat that follows is therefore something other than the hostilities that have been terminated. The category and the classifier are the same actor.

Kierkegaard observed that life can only be understood backwards. He did not propose that the constitutional framework of a republic could be governed backwards by an executive who classifies, in retrospect, what counted as the war. The May 1 letter takes a description of the human condition and turns it into a procedural instrument. The procedural instrument is what this dispatch is documenting. — Philosophy as procedure
Chapter IV

The Faustian Refrain

A second philosophical reference, drawn from a different tradition, sharpens the structural point.

The Greeks had a word for a specific kind of unchecked desire that they thought was politically corrosive: pleonexia. Aristotle placed it at the center of his political philosophy as the great destroyer of just communities. The pleonektes was not merely greedy in the vulgar sense. The pleonektes was incapable of self-restraint, incapable of recognizing limits, incapable of accepting that some part of what was available was not theirs to take. The disposition produced individuals who could not sustain friendships; it produced political communities that could not sustain justice.

Oswald Spengler, writing two and a half millennia later, identified what he took to be a specifically Western variant of this disposition: the Faustian drive. The defining symbol of Western culture, Spengler argued, was the infinite plane. The West reached for the unlimited; its mathematics invented calculus, its architecture built cathedrals that pointed to heaven, its empires kept expanding past any sustainable boundary. Christopher Dawson, the Catholic historian of culture, identified a related "missionary character" in Western civilization: a compulsion to transform the world rather than stabilize within it. Both men were describing the same energy. Neither fully addressed what happens to that energy when its outward channels are closed and the same hunger turns inward, becoming what one recent essayist has called "the Faustian drive fully unmoored."

The constitutional architecture of the American republic was built to channel this energy. The framers understood that an executive with the power to make war was an executive with the power to expand without limit. The constraint, they believed, lay in dividing the war-making authority between the executive (who could conduct war) and the legislature (who alone could declare it). The War Powers Resolution of 1973 was a late and partial attempt to revive this constraint after two centuries of erosion. It failed in operation almost immediately, but it failed by being ignored, evaded, or narrowly construed; not by being procedurally dissolved.

The May 1 letter completes the dissolution. It demonstrates that an executive with sufficient procedural creativity can simply declare the constraint inapplicable to the operation it would constrain. The hostilities have been terminated. The constraint no longer applies. The operations continue. The constitutional architecture, which was built to limit Faustian expansion through divided authority, is no longer a limit. It is a category that the executive can open and close at will, and that the institution responsible for resisting the closure has chosen not to resist.

This is what pleonexia looks like in a constitutional system. Not a tyrant seizing power, but an executive discovering that the procedural mechanisms designed to constrain its expansion can be dissolved by procedural innovation. The constraints do not need to be confronted. They need only to be classified out of relevance.

Chapter V

What This Adds

The original ten-dispatch series identified four variations of the temporal-mismatch pattern. Two subsequent dispatches added refinements. The Frozen Conflict identified a constitutional clock that had become dormant because Congress had chosen not to advance it. The Reshaping Tsunami identified a fifth variation: continuous transformation, in which the system functions while continuously rebuilding itself faster than the humans inside it can adapt.

The Terminated War identifies something different again. The constitutional clock is not dormant. It has been administratively reset. The institution that would advance it has not merely chosen not to act; it has been rendered procedurally inoperative by an executive innovation that operates faster than the institution can adapt to recognize it.

The 280-Character Cabinet, the sixth dispatch in the original series, described how state communication had migrated from formal institutional channels to platform-mediated content, with the institutions designed to deliberate on, constrain, or implement state policy unable to keep pace. The Terminated War extends that observation into the constitutional domain. It is no longer just that the president communicates at platform speed while institutions deliberate at institutional speed. It is that the president can now reclassify the constitutional category that constrains the action, at the same speed as the action, while the institution that would adjudicate the reclassification operates on a timescale that is structurally too slow to do so.

Senator Blumenthal's response captured the institutional helplessness precisely: "There's no pause button in the Constitution, or the War Powers Act." This is true. There is also no procedural mechanism for invalidating an executive declaration that hostilities have terminated when the executive controls the classification of what counts as hostilities. The institution can declare itself dissatisfied. It cannot, on the timescale at which the operations occur, do anything else.

Adam Smith, the ranking Democrat on the House Armed Services Committee, gave the more candid version: "Is the expectation that the Trump administration is going to follow the law? I do not have that expectation." The expectation has been abandoned. What replaces the expectation is the question of what the constitutional framework actually is when one of its central constraints has been dissolved by procedural innovation and the institution responsible for resisting the dissolution has chosen, openly, not to.

◆ The next dispatch the events will produce

The May 1 letter is a procedural innovation. The May 4 combat is the test of the innovation. The combat continues. The legal fiction holds. The next administration of either party will inherit the precedent that the War Powers clock can be reset by declaration. The next president who wishes to conduct an operation past the 60-day deadline will not need to invent the mechanism. The mechanism exists. It was built in 96 hours. It will outlast every actor currently using it. This is what a constitutional innovation looks like when it is built at executive speed and the response operates at institutional speed.

The Faustian energy that built the West, in the philosophers' analysis, found its limits in the constraints of finite resources, finite territory, and finite institutional patience. The constraints were always partial. They worked when the actors operating within them accepted that they applied. The question of what happens when an actor demonstrates that the constraints can be dissolved by procedural innovation, faster than the institutions designed to enforce them can respond, is the question this dispatch is documenting. The question does not have an answer. The events that produce the answer are still occurring.

The hostilities have terminated. The boats are sunk. Both statements are formally true within the administrative framework. The framework is the new fact. It will outlast the war it was built to manage, and the next war it manages will inherit it.

◆ End of dispatch

Postscript

This dispatch is shorter and more philosophical than its recent predecessors. It treats today's events as evidence rather than subject. The subject is the procedural innovation that the events have demonstrated to be operational, and the structural implication of that innovation for the constitutional framework that was supposed to constrain it.

The temporal-mismatch thesis has now been documented across fourteen dispatches. The pattern's variations are accumulating. Institutional slowness, institutional dismantling, epistemic erosion, the personal gap, continuous transformation; and now, in compressed form, the procedural-redefinition mechanism: an institution rendered inoperative not by the speed of the threat it was designed to address but by the speed at which the entity it was designed to constrain can reclassify the threat out of the category the institution would govern.

This is, in some ways, the most disturbing variation the series has produced, because it does not require the institution to fail. It requires only that the institution be slower than the actor it is designed to constrain, which it always is, and that the actor be willing to reclassify rather than confront, which the past several months have demonstrated is no longer a question. The institution does not need to be dismantled. It needs only to be reclassified out of relevance to the operation it was supposed to govern.

The remainder of this Iran war will be conducted under the legal fiction that the war has terminated. The next president, of whichever party, will inherit the precedent and decide whether to use it. The 96 hours between the May 1 letter and the May 4 combat are not the news. They are the precedent. The precedent will outlast the war.
