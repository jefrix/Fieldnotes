The Dual
Blockade

Two months into the war, the Strait of Hormuz has become a stalemate that neither side can win and neither side can afford to lose. The duration paradox is no longer a forecast. It is the situation.

Published April 28, 2026
Reading time ~24 minutes
Filed under Geopolitics / Economics
Sequel to №01 — The One-Year Threshold

Fifty-nine days ago, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury against Iran. Within hours, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps warned that any vessel attempting to transit the Strait of Hormuz without Iranian authorization would be targeted. Within days, hundreds of commercial ships dropped anchor, and the waterway through which twenty percent of the world's oil supply normally flows fell effectively silent. By March 31, only 292 commercial vessels had transited the strait under Iranian oversight; roughly ninety-five percent below pre-war levels, according to the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development.

Then two blockades settled over the strait, and neither has lifted.

Iran controls one. It has closed the waterway to vessels linked to the United States or Israel, established an escort system for approved ships, and begun charging transit fees of up to two million dollars per voyage; payments accepted in Chinese yuan and cryptocurrency, deposited into accounts at Iran's central bank. The IRGC Navy patrols the passage, assigns radio codes to authorized vessels, and routes them through Iranian territorial waters under armed escort rather than the standard international shipping lanes.

The United States controls the other. On April 13, following the collapse of peace talks in Islamabad, President Trump declared a naval blockade of all Iranian ports, directing the U.S. Navy to intercept any vessel entering or leaving Iranian coastal waters and to seize ships that have paid Iranian tolls. CENTCOM has turned back more than thirty vessels. The Navy has boarded and seized at least two tankers. On April 23, Trump ordered the Navy to "shoot and kill any boat" laying mines in the strait.

The first dispatch in this series, published on the war's eighth day, argued that the decisive variable in the Iran conflict would be duration, not intensity. It described the Duration Paradox: modern military firepower can destroy in days what takes years to replace, and the difference between a short war and a long one is structural rather than incremental. A short war is a shock. A long war is a condition. The line between them is the line between a system that recovers and a system that reorganizes around the damage.

Two months later, the war has crossed that line. The Strait of Hormuz is no longer a contested waterway. It is a stalemate; a dual blockade in which each side uses control of the passage as its primary leverage, neither side can open the strait without surrendering that leverage, and the global economy absorbs the cost of the impasse day by day, week by week, while the negotiations to resolve it operate on a timeline that is categorically slower than the damage they are trying to prevent.

Chapter I

The Toll Booth

Iran's decision to charge transit fees on the Strait of Hormuz is, from the perspective of international maritime law, illegal. Article 26 of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea prohibits states from levying charges on foreign vessels by reason of their passage through international straits. Articles 37 through 44 guarantee unrestricted transit passage that cannot be blocked, impeded, or made conditional. Iran has not ratified UNCLOS, but the convention codifies customary international law that predates the treaty itself, and the UK, France, and 169 other countries have explicitly rejected any legal basis for the tolls.

Iran's response has been blunt. "We are now in a state of war," Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi told reporters, "and wartime conditions cannot be governed by peacetime rules." The framing is strategic rather than legal: Tehran is not claiming a right to charge tolls under international law. It is claiming a right to charge tolls because it can. The IRGC Navy controls the passage. The mines Iran has laid (and, by some accounts, has lost track of) make the strait physically dangerous to transit without escort. The toll is not a fee for passage. It is a fee for survival, denominated in currencies that bypass the dollar-denominated financial system the United States uses to enforce sanctions.

The payments in yuan and cryptocurrency are a detail that deserves more attention than it has received. Bloomberg reported that some vessels have been required to change their registration and fly the flag of Pakistan before being escorted through. The IRGC has accepted payments through informal channels that are not systematically documented. The currency choices (yuan for state-aligned shippers, crypto for everyone else) represent a working alternative to the SWIFT-based payments infrastructure that the West has used as a tool of economic coercion since the Russian sanctions of 2022. Iran is not merely collecting revenue. It is demonstrating that the strait can function as an economic corridor outside the dollar system, and the demonstration has an audience that extends well beyond the current conflict.

This is the Toll Booth scenario that the fifth dispatch in this series described on the day the blockade was announced; but it has evolved beyond what that dispatch anticipated. The fifth dispatch treated the blockade as a single American action met by allied refusal. What has emerged instead is a dual structure in which both sides are blocking the strait simultaneously, for different purposes, using different mechanisms, and neither can relent without losing its primary source of leverage over the other.

Chapter II

The Stalemate

The negotiating positions are irreconcilable on the timeline that matters.

Iran's latest proposal, as reported by regional officials familiar with the closed-door discussions, would reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for the United States lifting its blockade of Iranian ports and agreeing to a long-term or permanent truce. The proposal explicitly defers negotiations on Iran's nuclear program to a future date. It is silent on Iran's missile program and its support for regional proxies. It offers, in effect, strait for blockade: you stop squeezing us, we stop squeezing everyone else.

The United States will almost certainly reject this proposal, because it does not address the issue that Trump cited when he ordered the bombing on February 28: ensuring that Iran cannot build an atomic weapon. The IAEA has reported that Iran possesses over 450 kilograms of sixty-percent enriched uranium, enough for nine to eleven nuclear weapons, with a breakout time measured in days. Trump has demanded that Iran "dig up and remove" all deeply buried nuclear material. Secretary of State Marco Rubio has said the United States will not accept a post-war arrangement in which Iran can monetize the strait. The American position is: dismantle the nuclear program, surrender the strait, and then we talk about sanctions relief.

Between these two positions lies a gap that cannot be closed at the speed the situation requires. The Council on Foreign Relations assessed the stalemate on April 24: "Both President Trump and the Iranian leadership think they are winning, so we have a stalemate over the strait. Trump does not want to lift the blockade until Iran agrees to a certain set of conditions; the Iranians won't agree." CFR's Steven Cook added that he is "skeptical" that the blockade will squeeze Iran enough to make its leadership more flexible, "given that they are hardliners and have been unwilling to capitulate on the battlefield or the negotiating table."

The ceasefire has been extended indefinitely. Trump announced the extension on April 21, saying that Iran's "fractured" leadership needed more time to submit a unified proposal. He simultaneously said the naval blockade would remain in place and that he was ready to resume bombing if talks did not progress. Iran's top negotiator warned that Tehran will not negotiate "under the shadow of threat" and that it has prepared "new cards on the battlefield." Pakistan, which has been mediating, took down all the security checkpoints it had erected in Islamabad in anticipation of a second round of talks. The second round has not materialized.

◆ The temporal structure of the stalemate

The war inflicts damage at the speed of economics: every day the strait is closed, oil prices remain elevated, jet fuel costs rise (Transport & Environment estimates an additional $104 per passenger on long-haul European flights), supply chains degrade, and both Iran's export economy and the global energy market absorb losses that compound. The negotiations to end the war operate at the speed of diplomacy: proposals, counter-proposals, collapsed talks, rescheduled deadlines, indefinite extensions, Pakistani intermediaries, train-traveled envoys. The damage accumulates daily. The resolution process operates in rounds separated by weeks. The gap between the two speeds is where the global economy is bleeding.

Chapter III

The Duration Paradox Confirmed

Return to the first dispatch. Its central argument was that the Iran war's critical variable would be duration, not the opening-phase military results. "Modern firepower can destroy in days what takes years to replace," the dispatch argued. "The difference between a short war and a long one is structural rather than incremental."

Two months later, the argument has been confirmed by the events it predicted.

The opening phase succeeded on its own terms. Iranian air defenses were degraded. Missile infrastructure was struck. Key figures in the IRGC and Iran's nuclear program were removed. The supreme leader was assassinated. By traditional military metrics, the campaign achieved its initial objectives within weeks.

None of that has produced a political resolution. The military success has not translated into a negotiating advantage sufficient to end the conflict, because the war's center of gravity shifted (exactly as the first dispatch warned it would) from the battlefield to the economy. The Strait of Hormuz is the mechanism through which that shift occurred. Iran cannot match American military power. But it does not need to. It needs only to control twenty percent of the world's oil supply and wait for the economic pressure to become intolerable; not to Iran (which has endured sanctions for decades) but to the global economy that depends on the strait remaining open.

The first dispatch described this dynamic as a race between two clocks: the military clock (which the United States wins by default) and the economic clock (which favors whoever can endure longer). Two months in, the military clock has stopped ticking; the ceasefire has frozen the battlefield. The economic clock is now the only one running, and it is running against everyone simultaneously. Iran's oil exports are strangled by the American blockade. The global economy is starved of Gulf energy by the Iranian blockade. The question is no longer who can destroy more but who breaks first under the pressure of what the destruction has already set in motion.

Chapter IV

The Expanding Radius

The duration paradox does not operate in isolation. The longer the stalemate persists, the wider the radius of its consequences; and in the past two weeks, the radius has expanded into domains that the first dispatch did not anticipate.

The second chokepoint. Iran-linked operations in the Red Sea, building on the Houthi precedent of 2024, are threatening a second maritime chokepoint off Somalia. If the Bab el-Mandeb strait becomes contested simultaneously with Hormuz, the two closures would bracket the Arabian Peninsula and effectively sever the shortest shipping route between Asia and Europe. The Amplification Channels dispatch warned that paired stresses are categorically more dangerous than single ones. A dual-chokepoint closure would be the maritime equivalent of the Ugarit Warning's paired-failure model.

The energy cascade. The strait's closure has triggered a uranium supply crunch (Iran is a significant source of enriched uranium for civilian reactors) and record-high jet fuel exports from the United States as Europe scrambles for alternative supply. Europe faces what analysts are calling a potential summer jet fuel crisis. The UK government has publicly acknowledged that the conflict will significantly impact its economic growth forecasts. Europe is accelerating its shift toward nuclear and solar energy; not as a long-term climate strategy but as an emergency response to the immediate loss of Gulf gas and oil. The energy transition, which was supposed to happen over decades, is being compressed by the war into months.

The alliance fracture deepens. Italy and other EU members face growing domestic pressure to suspend defense cooperation agreements with Israel, whose airstrikes in Gaza and Lebanon have continued despite the ceasefire. Israel's Prime Minister Netanyahu faces a unifying domestic opposition. China has blacklisted EU defense firms over Taiwan dealings and continues to curb U.S. investment in tech companies, accelerating the decoupling that the war has made strategically urgent. The alliance architecture that was already fraying under the strain described in the fifth dispatch has not recovered. It has continued to fracture along the lines that dispatch identified.

The domestic pressure. Two days ago, a gunman opened fire at the White House Correspondents' Dinner in Washington, attempting to assassinate the president. The suspect's writings criticized the administration's handling of the Iran war. The shooter was subdued by Secret Service; no attendees were seriously injured. Within minutes, conspiracy theories flooded both Bluesky and X; claims that the shooting was staged, that it was designed to justify Trump's proposed White House ballroom, that it was orchestrated by foreign intelligence. The PBS News analysis of the event noted that unfounded theories proliferated despite the presence of hundreds of professional journalists providing real-time factual accounts from the scene. The Liar's Dividend, which the ninth dispatch described in the context of election deepfakes, is now visible in the context of political violence: the mere possibility of fabrication allowed both sides to dismiss evidence that contradicted their preferred narrative.

The stalemate does not stay in the strait. Duration radiates. It radiates into energy markets, into alliance structures, into domestic politics, into the epistemic environment. Every week the dual blockade persists, the radius of its consequences expands into another system that was not designed to absorb the pressure. — The expanding radius
Chapter V

The Speed of Damage, the Speed of Diplomacy

The temporal mismatch in the dual blockade operates on the same structural logic as every previous dispatch in this series, but the specific mechanism is worth stating precisely because it is the clearest example the series has produced.

The damage from the blockade accumulates continuously. Every day the strait is closed, the global oil price remains elevated. Every day it remains elevated, airlines pay more for jet fuel, shipping companies reroute around the Cape of Good Hope (adding weeks and millions in costs per voyage), manufacturing supply chains degrade, fertilizer prices rise, food costs increase in import-dependent economies, and the compounding economic losses grow. The damage function is continuous. It does not pause for weekends, holidays, or diplomatic recesses.

The diplomatic process operates in discrete rounds. The first round of talks (Vance, Witkoff, and Kushner in Islamabad, April 11) lasted twenty-one hours and produced no agreement. The second round was canceled when Iran's foreign minister left Pakistan and Trump told his envoys not to travel. A third round has not been scheduled. Between rounds, weeks pass. During those weeks, the damage continues to accumulate at its daily rate while the diplomatic process is inert.

This is the temporal mismatch rendered in its purest economic form. The problem degrades continuously. The solution advances in intermittent steps separated by intervals of inaction. The gap between the two; the accumulated damage during the intervals when no negotiations are occurring; is the cost of the mismatch, and it is being paid by every economy that depends on Gulf energy, which is to say by the global economy as a whole.

The first dispatch predicted this. It argued that "every month spent sustaining a high-intensity campaign in the Middle East narrows Washington's flexibility elsewhere." Two months in, the narrowing is visible. The United States is maintaining a naval blockade that requires continuous deployment of carrier strike groups, guided-missile destroyers, and support vessels in the Persian Gulf and the Arabian Sea. These assets are not available for other contingencies. The duration paradox's second-order prediction; that a prolonged Middle Eastern engagement would weaken deterrence in the Indo-Pacific; has not yet been tested by a Chinese move, but the conditions for that test are present and worsening with each week the blockade continues.

Chapter VI

What This Dispatch Confirms

This is the first dispatch in the series that returns to a domain a previous dispatch has already examined. It does so because the events have confirmed the prediction, and the confirmation is itself a data point about the thesis.

The One-Year Threshold argued that the Iran war's decisive variable would be duration. Two months later, the war is a stalemate whose outcome depends entirely on which side can endure the economic pressure of the dual blockade longer. The military phase is frozen. The economic phase is the only one running. Duration has become the entire war.

The Toll Booth and the Tripwire argued that the blockade would fracture the Western alliance. Two months later, Italy is under domestic pressure to suspend defense cooperation with Israel, the UK has publicly acknowledged economic damage from the conflict, and the second round of peace talks collapsed without occurring. The alliance fracture has not healed. It has deepened.

The Amplification Channels argued that the financial system could absorb the initial shock but might not survive a second. The second shock has not yet arrived in the form the IMF warned about (a failed Treasury auction, a major NBFI deleveraging event) but the conditions for it are present and worsening: elevated oil prices, rising inflation, disrupted supply chains, increased insurance premiums for Gulf shipping, and a global economy absorbing the cost of the world's most important energy chokepoint being ninety-five percent closed for two months.

The thesis across all of these dispatches has been that complex systems fail when the threat arrives faster than the institutions designed to absorb it can respond. The dual blockade is the clearest confirmation: the economic damage accumulates daily; the diplomatic process to resolve it operates in rounds separated by weeks; and the gap between those two speeds is where the global economy is absorbing losses that compound with every day the stalemate persists.

◆ End of dispatch

Postscript

This dispatch is a sequel rather than a new entry in the series. It returns to the domain of the first dispatch to show what the Duration Paradox looks like when the paradox has resolved itself into fact.

The first dispatch predicted that duration would be the decisive variable. The fifth predicted that the blockade would fracture the alliance. The seventh predicted that the financial system's buffers were being drawn down. Two months of events have confirmed all three predictions, not because the author was prescient but because the structural dynamics the dispatches described were visible in the data at the time of writing and have simply continued operating along the trajectories they were already on.

This is what the temporal-mismatch thesis looks like when applied over time rather than across domains. The pattern does not just appear in different systems simultaneously. It compounds within a single system as duration extends the gap between the speed of the damage and the speed of the institutional response. The longer the stalemate persists, the wider the gap grows, and the wider the gap grows, the more systems are pulled into the radius of the damage.

The strait is the gap made visible. The oil that is not flowing through it is the cost of the mismatch, measured in barrels per day. The negotiations that are not occurring are the institutional response, measured in rounds that have not been scheduled. The distance between the two is growing. It has been growing for fifty-nine days. It will continue growing until someone decides that the cost of the stalemate exceeds the cost of concession, and that decision is being made at diplomatic speed in a crisis that compounds at economic speed.
